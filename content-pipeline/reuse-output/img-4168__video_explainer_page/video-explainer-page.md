# The Retirement Question Most People Never Get Asked

---

## Short Answer

Before you retire, it pays to pause and ask: beyond the numbers, what do I actually want my life to be when the career chapter closes? Most people have never been pressed to answer that. The math matters, investments, taxes, income, but it is only half the plan.

---

## Why This Question Matters

True retirement readiness is not just about knowing the numbers. It is about finding purpose, rhythm, and meaning in the next chapter when work is no longer the center of your days. For many, the switch from structure and purpose at work to “what comes next?” is the single biggest adjustment of retirement.

I see it often: people arrive asking how and when they can retire, or if they have enough. What they have rarely considered is what life will actually look and feel like the Monday after their last day at work.

---

## The Story Behind the Lesson

I recently sat down with an executive in this exact spot. They had done well, grown their business, checked off all the big financial boxes, and were looking for guidance for the next phase. We could have spent all our time on tax, investments, and estate work, but instead, I asked: When September comes and you retire, what does your Monday actually look like?

What started as a practical question opened something deeper. We talked for ninety minutes, just working through what they hoped their days and life might look like after work. For them, it was the first real time they had named it out loud. You could feel the emotion. For a lot of people, that is the first honest conversation about their own future outside of work.

---

## What the Usual Framing Misses

Most retirement planning is framed as: Do I have enough? Will the numbers work?
But the harder question is: What will give me purpose and satisfaction after work is over?
A list of things to do is not the same as a life you want to live.

Skipping this question can leave people with all the right numbers but a blank calendar, or feeling unsettled even if the projections say “yes.” Mondays become the hard part.

---

## The Classroom Version

If you are closing in on retirement or even just thinking about it, try this exercise:
- Imagine it is your first Monday after career. What will you do?
- Where will you find meaning and structure when there are no meetings, deadlines, or emails to answer?
- If your calendar looks empty, does that thrill you or unsettle you?

This is not just about staying busy. It is about identifying what you want to learn, build, create, give, or enjoy in this phase. The answers are different for everyone.

---

## Questions to Ask Yourself

- What are three things I want to look forward to when I no longer have work obligations?
- Are there relationships, hobbies, or causes I want to invest in, but haven't had space for?
- When I picture myself five years into retirement, what would make me proud or fulfilled?
- If I had to design a “perfect” week, what would that look like?
- Who will I spend my time with? Does my spouse or partner see this the same way?
- Am I avoiding this question because it feels uncomfortable or unknown?

---

## FAQ

**Q: Why does this question even matter if the numbers work?**
A: Because money is only part of the picture. Many people find themselves restless, unhappy, or surprised by how open their days feel once work stops. Planning ahead for purpose and rhythm can make the transition more successful and rewarding.

**Q: Is it normal not to know the answer right away?**
A: Absolutely. Most people have not been asked the question, or have not thought past the financial side. Sometimes it helps to talk it out and see what surfaces.

**Q: Can financial planning help with this, or is it personal?**
A: This is mainly a personal question, but your financial plan can help model possibilities and support what you want your days to look like. Sometimes it takes a few focused conversations for people to get clear.

---

## Useful Terms

- **Retirement transition:** The shift from full-time work into retirement, including both financial and lifestyle changes.
- **Purpose risk:** The chance of losing a sense of meaning or direction in retirement, even if financially secure.
- **Calendar blankness:** The sense of emptiness or lack of structure many feel after leaving daily work routines.
