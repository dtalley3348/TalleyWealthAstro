# Talley Wealth Target-Market Alignment Audit

Last updated: June 8, 2026

Source-of-truth relationship: this audit governs the public-positioning layer of Talley Wealth audience strategy. For the full layered model, use [Talley Wealth Market Architecture](./marketing/talley-wealth-market-architecture.md).

## Working Frame

Talley Wealth helps people make clearer decisions when retirement, taxes, investments, and business strategy all start affecting each other.

## Adversarial Positioning Debate

### Single-Niche Purist

Argument: choose one flagship market so prospects feel Talley Wealth was built specifically for them. The strongest single-market candidate is near-retirees in Northeast Tennessee with $1M-$5M in investable assets.

Score:

- Fit: high
- Differentiation: medium-high
- Revenue potential: high
- SEO support: high
- Operational reality: high
- Risk of losing good prospects: high

Verdict: compelling, but too narrow for the actual business. It would underuse the business-owner/tax-strategy advantage and could make profitable owner prospects feel like visitors instead of core clients.

### Two-Lane Strategist

Argument: make two audiences visibly primary: near-retirees and business owners. They share the same underlying Talley Wealth promise: connected, tax-aware, plain-English planning when decisions affect each other. This creates focus without pretending the firm serves only one kind of excellent client.

Score:

- Fit: high
- Differentiation: high
- Revenue potential: high
- SEO support: high
- Operational reality: high
- Risk of losing good prospects: medium-low

Verdict: winner. This best reflects the business David actually described while reducing the equal-weighted audience sprawl on the current site.

### Broad-Market Defender

Argument: keep the current broad multi-audience structure because it preserves opportunity across healthcare professionals, inherited wealth, equity-comp executives, employer-benefit households, and complex transitions.

Score:

- Fit: medium
- Differentiation: medium-low
- Revenue potential: medium-high
- SEO support: medium-high
- Operational reality: medium
- Risk of losing good prospects: low

Verdict: useful as a support strategy, not as the lead position. Broad support pages should remain, but homepage and core messaging should not present every audience as equal.

## Decision

This decision is about the public center of gravity, not the entire operating model.

Default hierarchy:

- Primary: near-retirees and retirement-transition households, especially in Northeast Tennessee.
- Primary: business owners with tax, cash-flow, entity, owner-compensation, retirement-plan, and strategy complexity.
- Secondary: executives with equity comp and busy high earners who want capable delegation.
- Secondary/support: healthcare professionals, inherited wealth, employer-benefit situations, and complex transitions.

Tie-breaker: client fit and differentiation win unless revenue or SEO creates a strong dissent. No such dissent currently overrides the two-lane strategy.

## Layering Rule

Talley Wealth should not flatten every audience label into one target-market list.

- Public center of gravity: near-retirees/retirees and business owners.
- Operational service rhythms: retirement/retirees, business owners, and high earners/accumulators.
- Entry points: specific SEO, referral, seminar, guide, and campaign pages nested under the relevant planning lens.
- Keystone tiers: Personal, Owner, and likely Custom as scope/pricing language, not market identity.

The public website can support many specific pages without implying that every niche is an equal center of the firm. Specificity belongs at the entry point. Clarity belongs at the center.

## Language Rules

- Lead with clearer decisions, connected decisions, tax-aware planning, and plain-English strategy.
- Use "when the moving parts need one clear strategy" or "when decisions start affecting each other" instead of "piecemeal."
- Do not list four or more target markets as equal on the homepage.
- Preserve secondary audiences as "also fits" or support/search paths so good prospects do not feel unwelcome.
- Nest specific pages under a planning lens instead of presenting them as new equal markets.
- Treat high earners/accumulators as a real service rhythm but not an equal homepage lane unless David explicitly changes the public positioning.
- Treat Keystone tiers as engagement scope and pricing language, not target-market labels.
- Keep "coordination" as a supporting concept, not the only public pain.
