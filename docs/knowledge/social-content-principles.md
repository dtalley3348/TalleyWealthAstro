# Social Content Principles

Use this for Talley Wealth short-form video hooks, LinkedIn posts, email openers, ad concepts, and other content that needs to sound like David in public.

## Purpose

Social content should create recognition, not performance.

The viewer should feel:

- David understands the real planning issue underneath the surface question.
- The point is specific enough to be useful.
- The tone is direct, thoughtful, and human.
- The content is not a generic advisor tip dressed up as a hook.

Every prompt should pass through the point-of-view map before it is treated as worth recording. The job is not to produce a clever first sentence. The job is to make one of David's durable beliefs visible in a way a real person recognizes.

The default model is now:

**micro-audience -> real situation -> deeper problem -> turn -> talk-through**

Start with the person, not the topic. The video should feel like David is responding to someone with a real financial tension, not delivering a generic planning lesson.

Default durable-belief sources:

- `docs/knowledge/point-of-view-map.md`
- `docs/knowledge/interpretation-layer.md`
- `docs/knowledge/voice-guide.md`

## Audience Strategy

Talley Wealth has more than one market, but the public strategy is no longer an equal-weighted list of audiences. The main public focus is tax-smart retirement planning and business owner profit-to-wealth planning, with retirement tax-window topics treated as major retirement-transition content.

For now, keep the social presence unified and organize content by pillars instead of splitting into separate channels. Separate channels add operational drag before there is enough posting volume and audience data to justify the complexity.

Use this default allocation for prompt batches unless David changes the emphasis:

- 50-55% near-retirees, recent retirees, retirement paycheck, retirement tax-window, Medicare/IRMAA, Social Security, 401(k), and withdrawal-sequencing topics.
- 30-35% business owners, owner pay, tax strategy, cash flow, exit readiness, and turning business profit into personal wealth.
- 10-15% executives, equity comp, healthcare/high-income professionals, and busy delegators.
- Remaining space for general David POV, Keystone, planning philosophy, implementation, and client-experience themes.

Use content pillars to keep the message clear:

- near-retirees and recent retirees with tax, income, Medicare, Roth conversion, investment, and estate coordination questions
- business owners and people approaching a liquidity event, sale, or major transition
- high-income W-2 earners with concentrated income, tax, benefits, equity comp, or planning complexity
- families with estate, inheritance, charitable giving, or multi-generation planning decisions
- people who are financially successful but still feel uncertain, disorganized, or under-advised

These pillars should stay visible over time: retirement confidence, sequencing, implementation, tax timing, Roth conversion pacing, business-owner planning, inherited wealth, and plan-before-portfolio.

Each individual video should speak to one audience and one tension. The overall feed can serve multiple markets as long as each post is specific.

Do not let a batch feel like Talley Wealth is equally built for six different markets. A good secondary-audience prompt is welcome, but the batch should still make the retirement-transition and business-owner focus visible.

## Intentional Persona Lanes

Use these as prompt lenses, not rigid boxes. A person can borrow language from more than one lane, and the prompt should only use the parts that obviously fit.

### Tax-smart retirement transition

People in the five years before or after retirement who are trying to decide whether the last paycheck can stop, where the retirement paycheck comes from, what the 401(k) should be doing now, which tax windows are closing, and whether a spouse or family member could carry the plan if needed.

Good prompt triggers:

- a last-paycheck date is starting to feel real
- Social Security, Medicare, Roth conversions, RMDs, withholding, or withdrawal order are colliding
- the person saved well but has never turned accounts into income
- the person is afraid of either running out of money or underspending the healthiest years

### Business owner profit-to-wealth

Profitable owners whose business is working, but the owner still has to decide how profit becomes personal wealth. The prompt should respect the spectrum: a solo attorney, a medical practice with partners and staff, and a larger operating company can all fit parts of the lane, but they do not need the same language.

Good prompt triggers:

- owner pay, distributions, salary, reasonable compensation, or estimated taxes feel improvised
- too much cash is sitting in the business without a clear job
- the owner is profitable but still unsure whether personal wealth is actually building
- the business carries too many roles: income, net worth, retirement plan, identity, and future exit

### Secondary complexity lanes

Use these when the transcript or prompt has a real situation, not just because the person is wealthy: executives with equity compensation, inherited-wealth families, high-income professionals, charitable or estate-planning households, and successful families with too many disconnected professionals.

### Referral-partner lens

Some prompts should be simple enough for a CPA, attorney, HR leader, or friend to forward. These should sound like useful plain-English decision framing, not a pitch for Talley Wealth.

## Persona Fit Spectrum

Before writing the prompt, decide how strongly the persona applies:

- **Direct fit:** the prompt can speak plainly to the lane.
- **Partial fit:** borrow only the relevant language and avoid over-describing the person.
- **Adjacent fit:** make the prompt about the decision, not the persona.
- **No fit:** do not force the lane; keep it as general POV or hold it for another batch.

This matters most for business-owner content. Do not make every owner sound like a multi-partner practice with staff, payroll, and succession complexity. Do not make every retirement prompt sound like the same age, same account mix, or same fear.

Broad audience labels are not enough. "Business owners" or "near-retirees" can be the lane, but they are not yet a prompt. Before writing the opening situation, create a required Micro-Audience Brief:

- Who they are.
- What stage they are in.
- What just happened, or what decision is approaching.
- What they privately worry, believe, avoid, or suspect.
- What David sees underneath the surface issue.

Example standard:

- Weak: "Audience: business owners."
- Better: "A profitable business owner had a great year, paid a painful tax bill, and still feels like the business is not turning into personal wealth."
- Strongest: "A 45-year-old business owner with growing profits has a CPA and decent books, but after another large tax bill he quietly wonders whether he is building wealth or just feeding the business."

Revisit separate channels only if one audience clearly dominates, content frequency is high enough to support multiple feeds, or one market needs a very different brand posture.

## Voice

Use `docs/knowledge/voice-guide.md` as the foundation.

For short-form content:

- Start close to the real tension.
- Prefer plain declarative sentences.
- Avoid fake urgency.
- Avoid influencer voice.
- Avoid "discover/unlock/transform" language.
- Avoid turning every hook into a dunk on other advisors.
- Be willing to name emotional reality without becoming melodramatic.

## Opening Jobs

Different openings do different jobs. The opening can be a hook, but it can also be a recognizable situation. Situation-first usually fits David better because it creates the feeling of, "Oh, he is talking about someone like me."

Structural hooks:

- expose a coordination problem
- show how taxes, investments, estate, income, or insurance interact
- work well for Keystone and integrated planning topics

Teacherly hooks:

- clarify a misunderstood rule
- make a technical topic feel explainable
- work well for Roth conversions, Social Security, Medicare, NUA, asset location, and tax planning

Emotional hooks:

- name the feeling underneath the numbers
- work well for retirement anxiety, spouse conversations, spending, identity, inheritance, and aging parents

Contrarian hooks:

- challenge a common belief
- should still be fair and grounded
- work best when David can explain the nuance immediately after the hook

## Recording Prompt Format

Use this format for future batches:

```md
### 1. Short Working Label

Micro-audience:
[Specific person/couple/business owner. Include stage, trigger, private worry, and what they are trying to avoid.]

Opening situation:
[A recognizable scene this person could immediately see themselves in.]

Deeper problem:
[What is really going on underneath the surface question.]

Turn:
[A simple pivot David can use to move from the situation into the explanation.]

Talk through:
[The 4-7 beats David can riff on. This should create enough energy that he wants to explain.]

Working title direction:
[Optional. Not final.]
```

Working labels are for internal organization only. Final titles should usually come from Mallory after editing and listening to the actual transcript.

## Recording Workflow

The goal is not to script David word-for-word.

Best workflow:

1. Choose the durable pillar or audience lane.
2. Write the Micro-Audience Brief before writing any opening line.
3. Write the opening situation, deeper problem, turn, and talk-through.
4. David grades prompts by "makes me want to record" and "sounds like a real person," not title quality.
5. David records from the situation and talks in his own words.
6. Mallory edits the video, chooses final titles from the actual transcript, and returns transcripts when available.
7. Transcripts are triaged for reuse.

## Distribution Workflow

Keep distribution simple enough to repeat.

Default approach:

1. Record one strong short video.
2. Lightly edit it once.
3. Post the same core video across LinkedIn, Instagram, Facebook/Meta, and X/Twitter when allowed and appropriate.
4. Make only small platform-specific adjustments: caption length, first line, hashtags, thumbnail/title, and compliance disclosures if needed.
5. Track what earns the right kind of attention, not just the most views.

Do not over-customize every platform at the early stage. Volume, consistency, and learning matter more than perfect platform-native packaging.

Prioritize a repeatable daily or near-daily rhythm over an elaborate content calendar.

## Prompt Quality Test

A strong prompt should pass most of these:

- Would David actually say this?
- Does the micro-audience feel like a real person, not a demographic label?
- Is there a recognizable situation before the explanation starts?
- Is the stage, trigger, private worry, and avoided decision clear?
- Does David see something underneath the surface question?
- Does it create a real thought, not just curiosity?
- Is it specific enough to avoid sounding generic?
- Does it set up a natural ramble?
- Does the talk-through have enough energy that David wants to explain it?
- Does it avoid overpromising?
- Does it avoid cheap fear?
- Could a smart client hear it without feeling talked down to?
- Which durable belief is this prompt expressing?
- Does it preserve any heat, tension, or specificity from David's raw thinking?
- Would the explanation naturally reveal how David thinks, or only teach a fact?

## Transcript Triage

When transcripts come back from Mallory, triage each video into one or more of these buckets:

- **Post Only:** publish the video, but do not spend much time repurposing it.
- **Caption/Post:** turn the transcript into a caption, LinkedIn post, email snippet, or short written post.
- **Website Asset:** convert the strongest parts into a Learning Center Q&A, blog section, FAQ, service-page support copy, or internal-linking support.
- **Core POV Asset:** preserve strong David lines, themes, examples, or turns of phrase for future hooks, website copy, emails, and messaging.

Evaluate transcripts with these questions:

- Did the micro-audience feel real?
- Did David say something specific or reusable?
- Did the video reveal how David thinks?
- Did it connect to a durable Talley Wealth belief?
- Is it useful enough to become written content?

## What To Avoid

- generic advisor tips
- broad audience labels without a micro-audience
- topic-first prompts that could apply to anyone
- exaggerated certainty
- manufactured drama
- "secret strategy" framing
- correction residue from prior drafts
- overexplaining the hook
- clever lines that do not lead to a useful explanation
- perfect-sounding openings that do not make David want to explain

The opening situation should open the door. David's explanation should do the real work.
