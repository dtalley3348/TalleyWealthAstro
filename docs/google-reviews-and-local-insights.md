### Johnson City Client Patterns

David sees four common fit patterns in Johnson City:

1. Retirement is getting close. Some people feel it after turning 50; many feel it closer to actual retirement. They wonder what retirement would look like and how much each additional working year changes the picture.
2. Business owners whose profit has increased and taxes now feel painful. They are usually past startup, comfortable paying employees, and ready to build a professional team around them.
3. People who inherited significant money and do not know what all to do. A related situation is adult children or power-of-attorney decision makers helping parents in their 80s with advisor/financial decisions.
4. High-income professionals with little time who want to pay a fee and know the money side is handled.

### Bristol / Southwest Virginia Client Patterns

- Bristol people are proud of being independent.
- Bristol still has a community feel, even if the shared Bristol identity feels weaker than it once did.
- Some of the shared identity comes from outside misconceptions about Appalachia.
- Bristol, Virginia often identifies more with Southwest Virginia, which is distinct from the rest of Virginia and close to Northeast Tennessee culturally.
- Bristol, Tennessee tends to identify more with Tennessee and has somewhat more southern influence while still being mountain culture.

- Strong Bristol/SWVA prospect archetype: "millionaire next door" retiree who saved and invested well but has never worked with someone like Talley Wealth.
- Other fits: medical professionals, mining families, business owners approaching retirement or succession, and people who grew up modestly, now have meaningful money, and do not want to be talked down to.
- Bristol/VA and SWVA prospects often ask whether relocating "across the border" to Tennessee would save state income taxes.

## "Real Financial Planning" Positioning

David's read on the market:

- Most local advisors are big-box "investment" advisors, not true financial planners.
- If they claim financial planning, it is often a basic Monte Carlo retirement analysis.
- People usually do not feel the tax/investment disconnect as the initial pain point until examples make it concrete.
- The stronger action driver is that they are tired of investment-only advice and are searching for something they would call "real financial planning," even if they cannot define it.
- Prospects likely think real financial planning means a thorough guide to retirement, not unnecessary complexity.
- "Thorough" means looking at the situation from multiple angles: market timing, assisted living costs, tax strategy, estate documents, trusts, retirement income, and portfolio decisions.
- People want simple reassurance that their money is being handled in a sophisticated, intelligent way.
- Estate planning review inside Keystone is often a major perceived win because many people have outdated documents or only a will.
- The no-cost strategy session is important because David can often identify possible tax-saving strategies that make the value tangible.
- Another major promise is clarity: after Keystone, they believe they will understand when they can retire and whether they will be okay.
