Plan Before Portfolio

Why "What Rate of Return Should I Get?" Is the Wrong Investing Question

The return you target should come from the job your money needs to do, not from a number you pulled from market history or a neighbor's portfolio.

By David Talley, CFP®, EA Source asset: IMG_4167 Review status: draft

Short Answer

Before asking what rate of return you should get, ask two better questions: how long each dollar needs to last, and how much volatility you can realistically live with without making a mistake. Once those two filters are clear, the return target becomes an output of the plan.

A return target sounds like the center of investment planning. It feels measurable, clean, and easy to compare. But for most families, especially near retirement, it is not the first question. It is a later question.

If you start with "I need 8%" or "I should be getting 10%," you may skip the actual planning work. The better conversation starts with what the money is for, when it will be used, what risks the household can absorb, and how much market movement the client can endure without abandoning the plan.

The Situation Behind The Question

Picture a couple five years from retirement. They have saved well. They have several account types, a few old 401(k)s, taxable savings, and maybe a Roth account. They are trying to decide whether their portfolio is "doing enough."

So they ask the natural question: "What rate of return should we expect?"

That question is understandable. It is also incomplete. Some of their money may need to create income within the next year. Some may have seven to ten years before it is touched. Some may not be needed for decades. Those dollars should not all be judged by the same return target.

The Deeper Problem

Surface question What rate of return should I get on my investments?
Deeper question What job does each part of my money need to do, and how much uncertainty can I handle while it does that job?
Why it matters If the portfolio is built around a return target alone, the plan can become either too aggressive to stick with or too conservative to support the life it is meant to fund.

A Better Decision Framework

Instead of starting with a percentage, start with these filters.

1

Give The Money A Time Horizon

Money needed next year has a different job than money intended for your seventies, eighties, heirs, or future charitable giving. A good portfolio does not treat every dollar as if it has the same timeline.

2

Name The Volatility You Can Actually Live With

This is not just a technical risk score. It is behavioral. If a portfolio drop causes you to panic, sell, or lose sleep for weeks, the plan may need to be adjusted before the market tests it.

3

Match The Asset To The Job

Once the timing and volatility filters are clear, the investment universe gets smaller. You can begin deciding which mix of assets belongs in each part of the plan.

4

Judge Return Against The Plan

A return is not "good" or "bad" in isolation. It should be judged against the risk taken, the goal assigned to the money, the market environment, and the role that account plays in the full plan.

"The question is not what rate of return should I get next year. It is bigger than that."

A Simple Way To Think About Portfolio Jobs

Retirement money usually has more than one job at the same time. That is why one blended return number can be misleading.

Money job Typical planning question What matters most
Near-term spending What money might we need in the next 1 to 2 years? Liquidity, stability, and avoiding forced selling at the wrong time.
Mid-term income What will support withdrawals over the next 5 to 10 years? Balance between growth, income, taxes, and market sequence risk.
Long-term growth What dollars can stay invested for decades? Inflation protection, disciplined risk, and the ability to stay invested through volatility.
Legacy or flexibility What money may support family, charitable, or later-life goals? Tax location, estate coordination, and the household's broader priorities.

Why Average Returns Can Mislead You

Market averages can be useful context, but they are not a yearly promise. An investment can have a long-term average that rarely shows up in any single calendar year. Some years may be much higher. Some may be much lower.

That is why the more useful planning question is not, "Did I get the average?" It is, "Is this return pattern normal for what I own, the risk I agreed to take, and the job this part of the portfolio is supposed to do?"

Questions To Bring To A Planning Conversation

  • Which dollars might I need in the next 1 to 2 years?
  • Which dollars can stay invested for 7 to 10 years or longer?
  • How much of a decline could I tolerate before I would be tempted to sell?
  • Is my portfolio diversified on purpose, or just spread across accounts?
  • Does each account have a clear job inside the retirement plan?
  • Are taxes, withdrawals, and investment risk being reviewed together?

Risks And Limits

This framing does not remove investment risk. It helps make the risk more intentional. A plan-first portfolio can still decline. Markets can still surprise you. Tax rules, income needs, health costs, and family priorities can still change.

The goal is not to find a magic return number. The goal is to build a portfolio that can support the plan, explain the tradeoffs, and help you avoid making emotional decisions when markets move.

Follow-Up Questions

Should I ignore rate of return entirely?

No. Return still matters. It just should not be the first filter. Once the plan defines the timeline, risk level, income needs, and tax context, return expectations can become part of a better conversation.

What if my portfolio is earning less than the market?

First compare it to the portfolio's actual job and risk level. A conservative income bucket should not be judged against a stock index. A long-term growth bucket should be evaluated differently than money set aside for near-term withdrawals.

How do I know if my portfolio has too much risk?

Look at both the numbers and your behavior. If the portfolio creates enough stress that you might abandon it during a downturn, the risk may be too high for the plan to work in real life.

Does this matter more near retirement?

Yes, often. Near retirement, some dollars may be needed soon while other dollars still need long-term growth. That mix makes the time-horizon question more important.
