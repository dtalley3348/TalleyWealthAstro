# Interpretation Layer

Use this whenever Codex is turning client facts, prospect facts, or raw content ideas into something David may say, send, record, or publish.

The interpretation layer is the middle step between source material and polished output. It prevents generic AI writing by asking what is really going on before drafting.

## Core Rule

Do not move directly from notes to polished writing.

First identify:

- the real tension
- the person's self-concept
- the emotional posture
- the practical stakes
- the organizing thesis
- what David needs to confirm

Only then write.

## David Point Of View Filter

Use `docs/knowledge/point-of-view-map.md` as an active filter, not as background reading.

Before drafting website copy, hooks, emails, articles, referral language, or strategy notes, ask:

- Which durable belief from the point-of-view map is this really expressing?
- What is the surface question, and what is the deeper problem underneath it?
- Where is the coordination problem, sequencing problem, implementation problem, or confidence problem?
- What would make the client feel seen rather than merely educated?
- What would a generic advisor say here, and what would David say that is more precise?
- What line of heat from the raw material should be protected instead of polished away?

If no durable belief is involved, either the piece is probably too generic or the belief has not been identified yet.

Do not let polished language erase David's sharper source ideas. Lines like "the client being in the middle is not a fair place to be," "the plan dictates the investments," "a retirement answer alone doesn't change their life," and "two plus two sometimes equals 3.98" are not throwaway phrases. Treat them as signal.

## The Organizing Thesis

Every serious output should have an organizing thesis: one or two sentences that explain what this is really about.

Examples:

- This is not mainly an estate planning project. It is a balance-sheet coordination project where Roth conversions, lifetime giving, liquidity, investments, and estate percentages all have to stay in sync.
- This prospect does not need more financial facts. They need a framework for deciding which facts matter first.
- This social hook is not about teaching the whole Roth conversion strategy. It is about making someone realize their tax window is shorter than they think.

If the thesis is weak, the output will feel generic even if the facts are correct.

## Interpretation Questions

Use the version that fits the workflow, then adapt as needed.

For client/prospect work:

1. What brought them here?
2. What are they carrying emotionally?
3. What is the central planning tension?
4. What is their self-concept?
5. What changed or needs to change?
6. What are they likely resisting or misunderstanding?
7. What should they feel after reading or hearing this?
8. What does David need to confirm?

For social/content work:

1. What belief is this challenging?
2. What problem is the viewer already feeling?
3. What is the smallest useful insight?
4. What would David naturally say if a client asked this across the table?
5. Is this hook structural, emotional, teacherly, or contrarian?
6. What should the viewer think after the first sentence?
7. What should David ramble about after saying the hook?

## Facts, Inferences, And Uncertainty

Separate:

- supported facts
- reasonable inferences
- open questions
- source-bias warnings
- implications for the final output

Do not smooth over uncertainty just to make the writing sound confident. David's voice is confident because it is grounded, not because it pretends.

## David Ramble Loop

The best workflow is often:

1. Codex gives a strong interpretation.
2. David rambles corrections, nuance, and objections.
3. Codex absorbs the ramble.
4. Codex rewrites without leaving correction residue.

Do not quote David's correction back mechanically. Use it to make the next version sound like it was understood correctly from the beginning.

## Quality Check

Before drafting, ask:

- Am I summarizing, or am I interpreting?
- Have I found the client/prospect/viewer's actual lens?
- Am I forcing a template?
- What would frustrate David if a junior advisor wrote this?
- What would make this feel like David rather than polished marketing copy?
- Did I preserve the live tension, or did I sand it into generic financial-advisor language?
- Can this be traced back to a durable David belief?

If those answers are weak, stay in interpretation longer.
