## Core Positioning

Talley Wealth is the local firm for people whose financial life has become important enough, complex enough, or close enough to a major decision that investment-only advice no longer feels sufficient.

The firm should feel:

- thoughtful, not flashy
- precise, not sterile
- personal, not personality-cultish
- sophisticated, not complicated
- local and embedded, not small-town in a limiting way
- plain-spoken, not simplistic

The strongest market-facing idea is not "everything should be connected" in abstract language. Prospects do not usually say that. They are more likely to feel:

- retirement is getting close and they want to know what life could actually look like
- taxes are starting to feel too large or too confusing
- they inherited money and do not want to make a well-meaning mistake
- their compensation, business, or family situation has become harder to manage alone
- they want a real person they trust to help them see what matters and what to do next

The coordination message still matters, but it should usually arrive as the explanation, not the first pain point.

## Brand Thesis

Most people arrive with pieces: investments, tax returns, estate documents, compensation decisions, business questions, retirement projections, or inherited assets.

The Keystone Method(tm) exists to understand the whole picture, shape the pieces, add what is missing, and make the decisions fit together.

The tone should make people feel relief:

> "This person understands people like me. He can probably help me sort through this."

Not:

> "This firm has a generic process and a polished brochure."

## Audiences

The design-system zip correctly points toward affluent/complex households, but it is too narrow if it over-focuses on "five years from financial independence."

Primary audience families:

- pre-retirees and retirees
- business owners with tax complexity
- people managing inherited wealth or family financial transitions
- executives and professionals with equity compensation
- physicians and health-care professionals
- Eastman employees/retirees and other regional employer-based professionals, handled carefully so no employer relationship or endorsement is implied

The shared thread is not age or one niche. The shared thread is that money decisions have become meaningful enough that generic investment advice feels thin.

## Voice

Keep:

- plain English
- first-person where David's judgment or philosophy is central
- direct but warm
- calm confidence
- short, grounded sentences
- willingness to say what is not a fit
- no hype
- no exaggerated certainty

Avoid:

- "holistic"
- "synergy"
- "roadmap"
- "financial journey"
- "unlock"
- "empower"
- "optimize" as default marketing language
- "solutions" as a vague noun
- "peace of mind"
- "fee-only"
- "no commissions"
- "no product sales"

Useful phrases:

- real financial planning
- plan first
- plain-English tax strategy
- the decisions that shape what comes next
- enough going on that a more thoughtful plan would feel useful
- no-pressure Explore Call
- if it is not a fit, I will say so directly
- the plan tells us what the investments need to do
- tax, investments, retirement, and estate planning in one conversation

Use "connected" and "coordinated" carefully. They are accurate, but they are advisor-side words. Translate them into lived situations when possible.

## Hand-Drawn / Whiteboard Motif

The downloaded design system says no hand-drawn visuals. That is too strict.

David naturally draws and explains ideas on a whiteboard in real client meetings. A restrained hand-drawn layer can be very on-brand when it reflects how advice is actually explained.

Allowed:

- whiteboard-style diagrams
- lightly hand-drawn arrows or annotations
- sketch-like planning frameworks
- marker-style conceptual visuals
- rough diagrams that feel like David explaining something live

Guardrails:

- use sparingly
- keep it premium, not cartoonish
- avoid doodles for decoration only
- do not make financial planning feel childish
- use hand-drawn visuals to clarify thinking, not to make the brand quirky

Best use cases:

- Keystone Method concepts
- sequence of returns explanations
- tax planning examples
- retirement paycheck diagrams
- short-form video companion graphics
- workshop slides and leave-behinds

## Photography

Use real, warm, human photography whenever possible.

Strong image directions:

- David explaining at the whiteboard
- David in conversation
- Stephenee and team moments that feel natural
- office scenes with warmth and depth
- local/regional imagery only when it helps a location page feel grounded

Avoid:

- AI-generated finance stock scenes
- handshakes
- skyscrapers
- laptop-with-chart cliches
- overly polished corporate portraits
- dark, blurry, atmospheric images where the subject is not clear

## UI and Interaction

Useful from the zip:

- accessible contrast rules
- focus states
- button states
- form states
- testimonial/card patterns
- data visualization palette
- slide and print templates

Refinements for our site:

- card radius should usually stay restrained; do not make everything rounded and soft
- use cards for repeated items, testimonials, tools, and modals
- avoid stacking cards inside cards
- hero copy should be clear but not dense
- CTAs should be direct, but not pushy
- interactivity should make concepts easier to understand

Preferred CTAs:

- Schedule an Explore Call
- See how Keystone works
- See who this tends to fit
- Download the guide
- Read the article

Avoid vague CTAs like:

- Learn More
- Get Started, unless the context is already very clear
- Unlock Your Future
- Book a Consultation

## Compliance-Sensitive Rules

Never claim:

- fee-only
- no commissions
- no product sales
- guaranteed outcomes
- guaranteed tax savings
- endorsed by Eastman, Ballad, or any employer

Preferred:

- fee-based, with transparent fees
- fiduciary financial planning
- where appropriate, tax planning is coordinated with tax preparation
- testimonials reflect individual experiences and are not a guarantee of future results

Reviews:

- use real reviews only
- do not fabricate or paraphrase into fake testimonials
- use first name and last initial
- use page-relevant reviews where possible
- include testimonial disclosure language

Hypothetical examples:

- any case-study-like example must be labeled as hypothetical
- avoid implying a real client outcome unless it is actually a real, approved testimonial

Employer pages:

- employer names can be used cautiously as audience context
- language must not imply endorsement, partnership, special access, or official relationship

## Content Priorities

The website should build authority through:

- high-quality persona pages
- city pages that are genuinely distinct in experience and local context
- service pages that explain the relationship between planning, investments, and tax
- blog posts that are actually useful and skimmable
- calculators/tools that make abstract risks visible
- guides and email sequences that help people move from curiosity to trust
- video-first content repurposed into written pieces, clips, emails, and search assets

The design system's presentation and print templates should be especially useful for:

- retirement workshops
- downloadable guides
- one-page explainers
- educational seminar decks
- follow-up PDFs after Explore Calls or webinars

## How Future Work Should Use the Zip

Use the zip for:

- tokens
- visual consistency
- logo/assets
- deck inspiration
- a11y reminders
- component state ideas
- brand voice examples

Do not use it as gospel for:

- current homepage copy
- final audience strategy
- SEO page structure
- persona positioning
- exact website architecture
- "never hand-drawn" rule

When in conflict:

1. The user's direct guidance wins.
2. The current Astro website strategy wins over the old React-site-based examples.
3. Compliance wins over marketing clarity.
4. Brand clarity wins over cleverness.
