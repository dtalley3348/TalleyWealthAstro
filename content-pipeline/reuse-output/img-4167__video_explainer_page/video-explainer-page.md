# Why “What Rate of Return Should I Get?” Is the Wrong Question

**Audience:** Retirement transition | Pre-retirees, new retirees, anyone trying to design a portfolio that fits real life
**Author:** David Talley, CFP®, EA | Talley Wealth

---

## Short Answer

Most people start investing by asking, “What rate of return should I get?” The reality is, that’s not the question that matters most. The rate of return is the output of your plan, not the starting point. The right questions come before you even start talking about percentages:
1. How long do you need each dollar to last?
2. How much market swing can you honestly handle without panicking?

Only after you answer these can you talk about returns that fit your plan.

---

## Why This Question Matters

When you sit down to design a retirement plan, or even just want to check if your investments are doing "okay", it's tempting to want a simple number. But in real life, your plan has moving parts. Returns are unpredictable from year to year, and even the average rarely shows up as a clean number in your actual results.

Focusing on returns first skips past the key decisions that really drive whether your plan works or not. It’s like planning a road trip by asking how fast your car goes, without knowing the route or how far you have to go.

---

## The Story Behind the Lesson

Over the years, I’ve talked with a lot of people at retirement’s doorstep. The same question comes up: “What rate of return should I be targeting?” The hard truth is, even if you picked the “right” number, that’s not what will make or break your retirement.

Think about it: There are millions of investment options out there. The only way to get from the whole world of possible portfolios down to an answer that fits *you* is by filtering through two things:
- What job does each dollar need to do (when will you spend it)?
- What kind of volatility could shake you out of your plan?

This is where real planning starts.

---

## What the Usual Framing Misses

People want a “safe” return. But real investment returns do not come in a neat box. The S&P 500 might average 10% over decades, but that exact number almost never happens in any single year. Most years are way up or way down.

The “what rate should I expect” framing also makes it easy to forget about sequence risk (when returns arrive), inflation, and the natural discomfort of seeing your account move. More risk does not always mean better or safer outcomes for your plan, especially if you end up bailing out during a tough stretch.

---

## The Classroom Version

Picture you’re approaching retirement, and you have several buckets of money:
- Some dollars you need to spend next year.
- Some you’ll need in five to seven years.
- Some might not be touched for 20 or 30 years.

Each bucket has a different time horizon. The money you’ll use soon likely needs to be steadier, with less room for wild swings. The dollars you won’t need for decades can afford to ride bigger market ups and downs.

Given that, the question shifts from “What’s my rate of return?” to “Are my dollars lined up for the right job, with a volatility I can really live with?”

The right investment mix comes from that sequencing, not from chasing a number.

---

## Self-Check Questions

Ask yourself:
- Do I know how long I’ll need each chunk of my investments to last?
- Have I been through a market drop and how did I react?
- Could my plan still work if returns are uneven or slower for a while?
- Am I assuming some years will be down, even though the long-term might look fine?
- Do I need to revisit my plan if I feel uneasy during a downturn?

If these questions feel uncomfortable, that’s not a failure. It’s a reason to step back and check that your plan fits the way *you* actually live and react, not just what an average number promises on paper.

## FAQ

**Q: So, should I even care about my investment returns?**
A: Yes, but only after you know what job your money has to do and how much risk you’ll really take on. Returns are important, but they’re a result of your plan, never the plan itself.

**Q: Is a “normal” return really that rare?**
A: It might be surprising, but the S&P 500 has only landed in that historical 10, 11% average range a few times in over 100 years. Most years are much higher or lower. Volatility is normal; single-year averages are rare.

**Q: What if I want the highest return possible?**
A: Everybody wants more, but the highest returns nearly always come with bigger swings. If a downturn would push you to sell at the wrong time, that higher target can backfire.

**Q: Can an advisor help me figure this out?**
A: A good advisor can help you break the problem down: look at your goals, your timeline, your comfort with moves up and down, and then help you build a plan where the risk and return fit *you*, not someone else’s assumptions.

---

## Useful Terms

- **Volatility:** How much an investment goes up and down over time.
- **Time Horizon:** How long until you need to spend or use the money.
- **Diversification:** Owning different things so that not everything goes up or down together.
- **Sequence Risk:** The order of returns, bad years early in retirement can do more damage than if they come later.
